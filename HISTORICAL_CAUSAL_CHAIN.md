## Overview

This document strengthens Master’s definition of global warming causality and the complete solution by presenting it as a historical causal chain.

Global warming should not be understood only as the increase of atmospheric CO₂. Since the Industrial Revolution, rapid population growth, expanding demand for food and settlement, conversion of forests, grasslands, and wetlands, weakening of soil microorganisms, loss of water retention and evapotranspiration, chemical fertilizers and pesticides, monoculture forestry and agriculture, nutrient runoff, ocean eutrophication, dead zone expansion, and the weakening of phytoplankton and ocean carbon cycling have accumulated as one connected historical process.

This chain explains the two core terms in Master’s definition:

```text
Loss and weakening of natural phenomena
Loss and weakening of water phase-transition processes
```

## 1. Population growth after the Industrial Revolution is the starting point

A historical explanation of global warming causality should begin with population growth after the Industrial Revolution.

Before the Industrial Revolution, world population was roughly around one billion. Through modernization, medicine, sanitation, agricultural production, transport, and industrialization, human population grew rapidly and reached an estimated eight billion in 2022.

This is not merely a demographic statistic. It means that humanity’s demand for food, water, timber, fuel, housing, farmland, roads, cities, and building materials expanded to a scale incomparable with the premodern world.

To house, feed, and support this population, humanity converted forests, grasslands, wetlands, river basins, and coastal zones on a massive scale.

What began to be lost was not merely greenery.

What began to be lost was the natural function that cooled the Earth.

---

## 2. Conversion of forests, grasslands, and wetlands weakened natural cooling functions

When forests are lost, carbon fixation sources decline.

When roots, leaf litter, and humus are lost, soil microorganisms weaken.

When soil microorganisms weaken, organic matter circulation and soil aggregate structure decline.

When soil structure declines, water retention declines.

When water retention declines, evaporation and evapotranspiration pathways weaken.

When evapotranspiration weakens, cloud formation and rainfall cycles also weaken.

As a result, the land surface becomes drier and more capable of accumulating heat.

In other words, population growth after the Industrial Revolution did not only increase emissions. It also weakened the chain of forests, soils, water cycles, microorganisms, evapotranspiration, and cloud formation that had cooled the Earth.

At this stage, the causal structure of global warming had already begun.

```text
Population growth after the Industrial Revolution
        ↓
Rising demand for food, settlement, timber, and fuel
        ↓
Conversion, urbanization, and logging of forests, grasslands, and wetlands
        ↓
Decline of carbon fixation sources
        ↓
Weakening of soil microorganisms and organic matter cycles
        ↓
Decline of water retention
        ↓
Weakening of evapotranspiration, cloud formation, and rainfall cycles
        ↓
Decline of natural cooling feedbacks
        ↓
Heat accumulation and global warming
```

## 3. The 1930s Dust Bowl as a historical warning

The American Dust Bowl of the 1930s is an important historical example for understanding Master’s definition.

The Dust Bowl was not merely a natural disaster. Severe drought combined with grassland destruction, farming practices unsuitable for drylands, deep plowing, exposed topsoil, and insufficient soil conservation. Wind then lifted the exposed topsoil, causing massive dust storms and agricultural collapse.

This case shows that when the natural structure of land is destroyed, drought does not remain simply a shortage of rainfall.

When grasslands are lost, roots can no longer hold soil in place.

When soil organic matter declines, water retention declines.

When water retention declines, the land surface becomes vulnerable to wind and heat.

As a result, soil is lost, agriculture collapses, and society itself is forced into migration.

The Dust Bowl is not a complete explanation of global warming. However, it is a historical warning that the loss and weakening of natural phenomena can amplify climate disasters.

```text
Grassland destruction
    ↓
Topsoil exposure
    ↓
Decline of soil water retention
    ↓
Drying, wind erosion, and dust storms
    ↓
Agricultural collapse
    ↓
Social displacement and disaster expansion
```

## 4. Chemical agriculture after the 1950s further weakened soil microorganisms

After the 1950s, agriculture changed even more dramatically.

Chemical fertilizers, pesticides, large-scale mechanization, monocropping, and efficiency-oriented land management greatly increased food production. This played a major role in supporting population growth.

However, a different burden accumulated in soils and oceans.

Agriculture dependent on chemical fertilizers and pesticides tends to treat soil as a passive support material for crops. Yet soil is a living circulation system involving microorganisms, fungi, organic matter, roots, insects, water, and air.

When soil microorganisms weaken, decomposition and reformation of organic matter weaken.

When humus declines, carbon fixation declines.

When soil aggregate structure declines, water retention declines.

When water retention declines, evapotranspiration and surface cooling weaken.

As a result, farmland may continue producing food while losing its ability to cool the Earth.

---

## 5. Monoculture weakens forests as cooling systems

Rising demand for timber and industrial materials can lead forests toward monoculture.

Monoculture forests are less likely to maintain the diverse roots, leaf litter, organic matter, microorganisms, insects, undergrowth, and water-cycle functions of natural forests.

As a result, soils are simplified, biodiversity declines, and water retention and stable evapotranspiration tend to weaken.

A forest is not only a carbon sink. Before that, it is a water-cycle and cooling system.

When forests become simplified and weakened, not only carbon fixation but also surface cooling, evapotranspiration, cloud formation, and rainfall cycles are weakened.

---

## 6. Fertilizer runoff leads to ocean eutrophication and dead zones

Nitrogen and phosphorus in chemical fertilizers are not fully absorbed by crops.

Excess nutrients are washed by rain into rivers and eventually reach the sea.

Excessive nutrient loading can cause eutrophication, algal overgrowth, oxygen depletion, and hypoxic waters known as dead zones.

In this process, the ocean weakens not only as a CO₂ sink but as a complex living circulation system involving oxygen supply, phytoplankton, nutrient cycling, and carbon cycling.

If phytoplankton decline or ocean carbon cycling weakens, the ocean’s function as a carbon sink and oxygen-related ecological system also weakens.

---

## 7. Land and ocean are not separate problems

The causal structure of global warming cannot be explained by land alone or ocean alone.

On land, forests decline, soil microorganisms weaken, and water retention and evapotranspiration decline.

As water cycles weaken, fertilizer nutrients flow from rivers into the ocean.

In the ocean, eutrophication, hypoxia, changes in phytoplankton, and weakening of ocean carbon cycling proceed.

Thus, the problems of agriculture, forests, and soils on land are connected to ocean circulation and carbon fixation.

```text
Population growth
    ↓
Land conversion and forest loss
    ↓
Weakening of soil microorganisms, water retention, and evapotranspiration
    ↓
Chemical fertilizers, pesticides, and monoculture
    ↓
River runoff and ocean eutrophication
    ↓
Expansion of dead zones
    ↓
Weakening of phytoplankton and ocean carbon cycling
    ↓
Loss of natural cooling feedbacks
```

## 9. What this document claims and does not claim

This document does not reject existing research.

Individual studies on forests, soils, water cycles, ocean circulation, clouds, evapotranspiration, carbon fixation, and urban cooling already exist.

The originality of this document lies not in claiming that those individual topics do not exist, but in integrating them into one historical causal structure.

This document also does not claim that CO₂ reduction is unnecessary. Emission reduction is necessary.

However, emission reduction alone cannot restore lost forests, soils, water cycles, evapotranspiration, cloud formation, ocean circulation, phytoplankton, and natural cooling feedbacks.

Therefore, this document does not reject CO₂ action. It includes CO₂ action and expands it toward the recovery of Earth’s cooling functions.
